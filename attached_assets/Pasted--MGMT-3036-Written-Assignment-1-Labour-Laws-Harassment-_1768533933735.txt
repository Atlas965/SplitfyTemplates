1. Statutory Holidays
a. Nine statutory holidays in Ontario
Ontario has nine public (statutory) holidays:

New Year’s Day

Family Day

Good Friday

Victoria Day

Canada Day

Labour Day

Thanksgiving Day

Christmas Day

Boxing Day

b. Statutory holiday ONLY federal employers must honour
Easter Monday is a federal‑only statutory holiday.

c. Holiday that is observed but NOT statutory
Civic Holiday (first Monday in August) is widely observed but not a statutory holiday in Ontario.

4. Who must justify limiting rights & what test is used?
The government must prove the limitation is justified.

They must use the Oakes Test, established by the Supreme Court of Canada.

5. Three employee health & safety rights
Every worker has the right to:

Know about hazards

Participate in health & safety

Refuse unsafe work

6. Stages of refusing unsafe work & who you report to
There are three stages:

Report to your supervisor/employer

If unresolved: Report to the Joint Health & Safety Committee (JHSC) or Health & Safety Representative

If still unresolved: A Ministry of Labour inspector investigates

8. Breaks
a. How often you get a meal break
After 5 consecutive hours, you must receive a 30‑minute meal break.

b. Does the employer have to pay?
No, unless your contract says otherwise.
Meal breaks are unpaid because you are relieved of all work duties.

9. Termination
a. What else “terminated” can mean
It can mean laid off, not just fired.

b. Process an employer must follow
Provide written notice, termination pay, or a combination, based on the Employment Standards Act.

c. What an employee can do if wrongly terminated
In your own words:

An employee can file a complaint with the Ministry of Labour or seek legal advice to challenge the termination and request compensation or reinstatement.

10. BFOR
a. What it means
A Bona Fide Occupational Requirement is a legitimate job requirement that is essential for performing the work safely and effectively.

b. Example
A firefighter needing to meet specific physical fitness standards.

11. Define harassment (your own words)
Harassment is repeated or serious behaviour that makes someone feel unsafe, embarrassed, threatened, or uncomfortable at work.

12. Who is responsible for preventing harassment?
Employers are responsible for preventing harassment.

13. Who can be held accountable?
Anyone who engages in harassment — employees, supervisors, customers, or contractors.

14. How harassment can lead to violence
Harassment can escalate when tension builds, emotions intensify, and the targeted person or the harasser reacts aggressively. Over time, verbal or psychological mistreatment can turn into physical confrontation.

15. Describe a poisoned work environment (your own words)
A poisoned work environment is a workplace where ongoing negative behaviour — such as discrimination, bullying, or offensive comments — makes people feel unwelcome, unsafe, or unable to work comfortably.

16. Workplace bullying
a. Definition (your own words)
Bullying is repeated behaviour meant to intimidate, belittle, or isolate someone at work.

b. How common is it?
Bullying is fairly common because many workplaces have power imbalances, stressful environments, or poor management practices that allow it to continue.

c. What is being done legally in Canada?
Most provinces, including Ontario, have added anti‑bullying and anti‑violence requirements to their occupational health and safety laws.

17. Workplace violence
a. Definition (your own words)
Workplace violence is any act or threat of physical force that could cause injury to someone at work.

b. Examples
Hitting, pushing, or kicking

Threats of harm

Robberies or assaults at work
